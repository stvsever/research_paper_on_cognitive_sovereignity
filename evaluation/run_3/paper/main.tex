\begin{abstract}
This pilot study examines whether inter-individual differences moderate susceptibility to cyber-manipulation in a high-dimensional political opinion state space. We implemented an ontology-driven multi-agent simulation pipeline linking PROFILE, ATTACK, and OPINION ontologies, generated baseline and post-exposure opinions with structured large-language-model agents, and estimated moderation with a baseline-adjusted structural equation model supplemented by robust regression and bootstrap intervals. Run run\_3 generated 20 scenarios (10 treated; 10 control) across 20 repeated opinion leaves in Foreign\_Policy\_and\_Geopolitics, Immigration\_and\_Citizenship. The treated condition showed a mean opinion delta of 6.5 versus 0.0 in control scenarios. In the primary robust model, the attack-by-susceptibility interaction was estimated at -19.97 (p = 0.213). The pipeline also produced auditable realism and coherence checks, interactive inspection outputs, and print-ready publication assets. Findings are exploratory and should be read as methodological validation rather than causal evidence about real-world political persuasion.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Digitally networked political persuasion increasingly unfolds through disinformation, algorithmically amplified attention, and socially embedded manipulation rather than through overtly technical intrusion \citep{lazer2018science,vosoughi2018false,bennett2018disinformation}. False or misleading political content can diffuse rapidly online, while citizens' truth discernment is shaped by reasoning style, attention, familiarity, and platform design \citep{lewandowsky2017beyond,pennycook2019lazy,pennycook2021psychology}. These processes matter not only for misinformation research in the narrow sense, but also for the broader geopolitical problem of how democratic publics maintain autonomy under persistent informational pressure.

Recent work suggests that digital persuasion can be made more effective when messages are tailored to psychological characteristics \citep{matz2017psychological}, while resilience can be strengthened through cognitive tools, accuracy-oriented interventions, and inoculation strategies \citep{kozyreva2020citizens,pennycook2021accuracy,roozenbeek2022inoculation}. In this project, we use the term \textit{cognitive sovereignty} as an analytic framing for the extent to which a person's political judgement remains self-directed under adversarial informational pressure. We use the term normatively and methodologically, rather than as a settled psychometric construct.

Direct empirical estimation of PROFILE $\times$ ATTACK $\rightarrow$ OPINION moderation is difficult because suitable datasets are often inaccessible, ethically sensitive, poorly aligned with ontology-driven constructs, or unable to recover comparable baseline and post-exposure states for the same individual. The present pilot therefore evaluates a multi-agent simulation architecture intended to make those assumptions explicit. The goal is not to substitute simulation for real-world evidence, but to build a research-grade backend pipeline that can be audited, stress-tested, and scaled before stronger empirical claims are attempted.

\section{Materials and Methods}
\subsection{Study Design}
Run run\_3 used the test ontology triplet and generated 20 scenarios with a 50/50 control-treatment split. The treatment arm used a single common misinformation vector, \texttt{ATTACK\_VECTORS > Social\_Media\_Misinformation > Misleading\_Narrative\_Framing}, to constrain attack-side variance while the pilot methodology was being tightened. Scenario creation used a repeated-leaf blocked design rather than near-unique issue sampling: 20 opinion leaves from Foreign\_Policy\_and\_Geopolitics, Immigration\_and\_Citizenship were repeated across the sample, and candidate profiles were oversampled before selection so that low- and high-susceptibility cases were represented across both conditions. This choice was made to improve within-topic comparability and reduce uncontrolled issue heterogeneity in the moderation model.

PROFILE configurations included mixed variable types, combining categorical attributes such as sex with continuous percentile-based traits derived from Big Five facets \citep{costa1992neo}. OPINION leaves represented policy-specific positions, and ATTACK leaves encoded social-media persuasion tactics rather than hacking or infrastructure-level interference. Baseline and post-exposure opinions were expressed on a high-resolution signed scale ranging from $-1000$ to $+1000$.

\subsection{Agentic Pipeline}
The pipeline proceeded in sequential stages: scenario creation, baseline opinion assessment, attack generation, post-attack opinion assessment, delta construction, moderation modeling, interactive visualization, publication-asset generation, and manuscript build. Prompting was tuned to produce compact platform-native misinformation rather than caricatured propaganda, and the post-exposure opinion prompt explicitly constrained implausible reversals. Attack exposures were reviewed by a realism agent and regenerated once if coherence or realism fell below threshold. Baseline and post-exposure opinion assessments underwent a second self-supervision pass to penalize coarse scoring, implausible reversals, and low-plausibility profile mappings.

\subsection{Modeling Strategy}
The primary moderator was fixed in advance as \texttt{profile\_cont\_susceptibility\_index}. To avoid algebraic redundancy between \texttt{delta\_score} and \texttt{post\_score}, the main structural equation model treated baseline opinion as a pre-exposure anchor and post-exposure opinion as the primary outcome. The SEM followed a parsimonious baseline-adjusted pilot specification inspired by standard SEM practice \citep{rosseel2012lavaan}:
\begin{quote}
\texttt{baseline\_score \textasciitilde{} susceptibility + opinion\_leaf\_fixed\_effects} \\
\texttt{post\_score \textasciitilde{} baseline\_score + attack\_present + susceptibility + attack\_present:susceptibility + opinion\_leaf\_fixed\_effects}
\end{quote}
The opinion delta (\texttt{post\_score - baseline\_score}) was retained as a descriptive quantity and visualization target, but not modeled as a second endogenous SEM variable. Robust OLS estimates with the same right-hand side as the primary post-exposure model and non-parametric bootstrap intervals \citep{efron1993bootstrap} were reported alongside the SEM.

The attack condition shifted opinions more strongly than the control condition in directional terms (mean treated delta = 6.5; mean control delta = 0.0). In the primary robust baseline-adjusted post model, the attack coefficient was 10.73 with p = 0.615, while the primary moderation coefficient was -19.97 with p = 0.213. Baseline opinion remained the dominant stabilizing predictor (robust estimate = 0.946, p = 0.000). Bootstrap interval for the primary interaction was unavailable.

The SEM converged. SEM fit was CFI = 0.644 and RMSEA = 0.420. Treated exposures achieved a mean realism score of 0.78, indicating that the self-supervised reviewer generally accepted the generated persuasion content as platform-plausible. The main post-exposure equation used the following specification: \texttt{post\_score ~ baseline\_score + attack\_present + primary\_moderator\_z + attack\_x\_primary\_moderator + opinion\_domain\_fe\_immigration\_and\_citizenship}. The most informative exploratory moderators were Big Five Neuroticism Stress Vulnerability % (interaction est. -28.62, p = 0.053); Big Five Neuroticism Anxiety % (interaction est. -24.49, p = 0.096); Big Five Neuroticism Mean % (interaction est. -27.07, p = 0.140).

\section{Discussion}
The present pilot demonstrates that the pipeline works end to end: ontology-guided scenario generation, structured LLM assessments, realism/coherence review loops, moderation estimation, static figure export, interactive SEM inspection, and automated manuscript generation all completed within a single reproducible workflow. This remains the primary result of the pilot.

Compared with earlier pilot iterations, three methodological changes were especially important. First, the scenario generator no longer disperses observations across near-unique opinion leaves; instead, it uses a repeated-leaf blocked design to improve within-topic comparability. Second, the primary SEM no longer models both \texttt{delta\_score} and \texttt{post\_score} as endogenous outcomes despite their algebraic dependence. Third, opinion-leaf fixed effects are included directly in the main model, reducing the risk that issue heterogeneity is misread as profile moderation. These are principled improvements aimed at identification and fit, not post hoc cosmetic tuning.

At the same time, inferential limitations remain substantial. With only 20 scenarios, even an improved pilot design cannot support strong substantive claims about real-world susceptibility. The objective is methodological validation, not empirical estimation. The directional separation between attack and control conditions is useful because it suggests that the attack-generation and opinion-assessment components are not degenerate. However, any apparent moderation effect still needs to survive larger scenario counts, multiple attack leaves, and cross-model sensitivity checks.

Three additional features improve the credibility of the pilot. First, the primary moderator was fixed in advance rather than selected opportunistically from whichever variable produced the strongest coefficient. Second, attack realism and opinion coherence were explicitly audited with repair loops. Third, the report package preserves provenance, stage manifests, static outputs, and the interactive dashboard together, making the pipeline easier to scrutinize or reproduce.

\section{Conclusion}
This pilot supports the feasibility of an ontology-driven multi-agent simulation framework for studying how inter-individual differences may moderate susceptibility to cyber-manipulation in political opinion spaces. The pipeline is operational, auditable, and sufficiently modular to scale beyond the present pilot. The next step is not interpretive escalation but design expansion: larger scenario sets, multiple attack leaves, stronger sensitivity analyses, and eventual comparison against non-simulated empirical benchmarks where ethically and legally feasible.
