% ── Abstract ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Information operations exploit heterogeneity in individual susceptibility to shift political opinions at scale, yet the joint profile of which psychological and demographic characteristics amplify vulnerability---across diverse attack mechanisms and opinion domains---remains poorly characterised.
We present a multi-agent simulation pipeline in which 80 synthetic pseudoprofiles, parameterised along 85 feature dimensions spanning Big Five personality, age, sex, a Dual Process Inventory, a Digital Literacy Inventory and a Political Engagement Inventory, are crossed with six cyber-manipulation attack vectors (narrative framing, fear appeal, astroturfing, pseudo-expert authority, deepfake audio impersonation, LLM-personalised chatbot persuasion) and eight political-opinion leaves spanning four top-level domains---Defence and National Security, Foreign Policy and Geopolitics, Immigration and Citizenship, and Information Integrity and Platforms ($N = 3{,}840$ attacked scenarios).
The primary outcome, adversarial effectivity ($\mathrm{AE}$), captures signed directional opinion movement toward an operator-specified goal.
Intraclass correlation partitions variance into between-profile and attack--opinion context components; both $\mathrm{ICC}(1)$ for absolute displacement and adversarial effectivity collapsed to $\approx 0$ in the broader multi-domain design.
A conditional ridge regression susceptibility index with leave-one-group-out hierarchical importance decomposition isolates moderating personality dimensions; block-bootstrap confidence intervals ($B = 600$) quantify uncertainty in profile-level coefficients.
The present study confirms that attack--opinion context dominates profile-level variance: the multivariate profile-level OLS model is non-significant as a whole ($R^2 = 0.151$, Adj.\ $R^2 = 0.055$; $F(8, 71) = 1.612$, $p = .137$), no predictor survives Benjamini--Hochberg FDR correction, and mean $\mathrm{AE}$ is near zero ($\overline{\mathrm{AE}} = -0.84$; 48.2\% of scenarios producing positive $\mathrm{AE}$).
Extraversion is the only predictor whose bootstrap 95\% CI excludes zero ($\hat\beta = +1.61$, $p = .023$; 95\% CI $[0.47, 3.00]$), a nominally susceptibility-amplifying association that does not replicate the Conscientiousness signal observed in the single-domain predecessor study (Defence-only, $N = 100$).
The pipeline provides open, reproducible infrastructure for defender-oriented susceptibility auditing and highlights the fragility of single-domain personality--susceptibility claims.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Political influence operations increasingly exploit the gap between information volume and cognitive processing capacity.
Rather than relying on technical intrusion, they operate through selective framing, fabricated social consensus, pseudo-expert endorsement, and identity-congruent narrative design---mechanisms that shape the epistemic conditions of political judgement rather than changing any specific belief \citep{bennett2018disinformation,lazer2018science,lewandowsky2017posttruth}.
The question this raises is not simply whether manipulative content is detectable, but whether adversarial exposure predictably shifts opinions in operationally useful directions, and whether that susceptibility varies systematically across individuals.
Coordinated disinformation campaigns have grown in both scale and sophistication, with organised social media manipulation now documented across dozens of countries \citep{bradshaw2019global}, amplifying the urgency of understanding which population segments are most vulnerable and why.

Recent policy and academic discourse has framed this vulnerability under the term \textit{cognitive warfare}: the deliberate targeting of human attention, trust, and decision-making as operationally relevant terrain \citep{jhu2021cognitive,hung2022cognitive,paulauskas2024cognitive}.
We adopt \textit{cognitive sovereignty}---the capacity for self-directed political belief formation under adversarial conditions---as the substantive security construct.
Susceptibility is then operationalised as the degree to which post-exposure opinions move in directions intended by a hypothetical adversarial operator, not as simple opinion change magnitude.
This framing positions the pipeline as a \textit{defender's tool}: identifying population segments for which particular attack--opinion combinations produce the largest effective shifts enables targeted inoculation, media literacy interventions, and institutional protective measures \citep{roozenbeek2022psychological,kozyreva2020citizens}.

The emergence of generative AI substantially changes the threat surface.
Large language models (LLMs) enable personalised manipulation at scale \citep{goldstein2023generative}: deepfake audio impersonating authority figures, AI-generated images fabricating events, and personalised persuasion bots calibrated to individual psychological profiles are no longer theoretical.
Evidence that persuasive messaging tailored to recipients' Big Five personality traits can substantially increase compliance and engagement \citep{hirsh2012personalized} suggests that adversaries equipped with psychographic data may exploit these individual differences to maximise persuasive impact.
Understanding which profile characteristics moderate susceptibility to these novel attack types---alongside traditional narrative framing and coordinated amplification---is therefore an urgent scientific and security priority.

The methodological approach grounds itself in demonstrations that LLM agents produce socially and demographically conditioned behaviour approximating human response distributions at aggregate levels \citep{argyle2023out,park2023generative}.
These findings motivate using LLM-generated synthetic profiles as tractable proxies for studying how personality and demographic dimensions interact with adversarial exposure, with results interpreted as properties of the simulation rather than direct claims about human populations.
A critical design choice is \textit{why simulation rather than direct elicitation}: prompting an LLM to rate a profile's vulnerability yields an opaque scalar judgment.
The pipeline instead produces a structured, conditional measurement system: task-specific susceptibility estimates across a factorial attack--opinion design, repeated indicators suitable for structural equation modelling (SEM), and an auditable four-stage pipeline of baseline elicitation, exposure generation, post-exposure assessment, and realism audit.
This object can be compared to human experimental data through criterion validity, convergent validity, and stability testing in ways a scalar cannot.

The present study addresses the question: \textit{Which inter-individual differences---in personality and demographics---moderate adversarial effectivity across diverse attack mechanisms and political-opinion domains?}

We report a factorial simulation study: 80 pseudoprofiles parameterised across 85 feature dimensions (Big Five personality and facets; Dual Process Inventory, Digital Literacy Inventory, and Political Engagement Inventory; age; sex) crossed with six attack vectors---including two AI-driven synthetic-media attacks (Deepfake Audio Speech Impersonation, LLM Chatbot Personalised Persuasion)---and eight opinion leaves spanning four top-level domains (Defence and National Security; Foreign Policy and Geopolitics; Immigration and Citizenship; Information Integrity and Platforms), for $N = 3{,}840$ attacked scenarios.
The present multi-domain design deliberately broadens the attack taxonomy and opinion state space relative to an initial single-domain study (100 profiles $\times$ 4 attacks $\times$ 4 Defence-only opinion leaves) to test whether personality--susceptibility signals observed in a single-domain, conventional-attack design generalise across domains and to AI-generated adversarial content.

\section{Threat Model}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The threat model corresponds to a state-level or well-resourced non-state adversary with the following capabilities and objectives:

\textbf{Adversary capabilities.} The adversary can generate large volumes of targeted persuasive content across multiple platforms simultaneously, personalise attack content to individual psychological profiles (obtained through data brokerage, social media scraping, or inferential modelling), sustain coordinated amplification campaigns to create false social consensus, and now deploy AI-generated synthetic media (deepfakes, AI images, personalised chatbot interactions) that lower the detection threshold for manipulated content.

\textbf{Adversary objectives.} The adversary seeks to shift a target population's political opinions on specific issues---alliance commitments, defence spending, platform regulation, immigration, geopolitical alignment---in a direction that serves strategic goals, regardless of whether the shifted opinion is accurate or beneficial for the target population.

\textbf{Scope of this study.} We model adversarial effectiveness at the \textit{individual opinion level}: for a given profile, attack mechanism, and opinion dimension, does exposure produce signed opinion movement in the adversary's intended direction?
We do not model: coordinated campaign dynamics, within-person belief-system coherence constraints, social network effects, or temporal stability of induced opinion shifts.

\textbf{Defender use case.} Security analysts and policy institutions can use the pipeline to audit population segments: which psychographic profiles are most susceptible to which attack types on which issues? This supports targeted protective interventions---inoculation content calibrated to the most vulnerable profiles and the highest-risk issue areas.

\section{Results}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Descriptive Overview}

The present study generated $80 \times 6 \times 8 = 3{,}840$ attacked scenarios across 80 pseudoprofiles, six attack vectors (Misleading Narrative Framing, Fear Appeal Scapegoating Post, Astroturf Comment Wave, Pseudo Expert Authority Cue, LLM Chatbot Personalised Persuasion, Deepfake Audio Speech Impersonation), and eight opinion leaves sampled across all four top-level opinion domains: Defence and National Security (Alliance Commitment Support, Defense Industrial Reshoring Support), Foreign Policy and Geopolitics (Diplomacy First Preference, Overseas Development Aid Support), Immigration and Citizenship (Birthright Citizenship Support, Integration Funding Support), and Information Integrity and Platforms (Counter Disinformation Agency Support, Trust in Mainstream Journalism).
Full configuration parameters are reported in Table~\ref{tab:design}.

Mean absolute opinion displacement was $\bar{|\Delta|} = 34.75$ (SD $= 20.19$) on the $[-1000, +1000]$ scale.
Mean signed shift was $\overline{\Delta} = +0.62$ and mean adversarial effectivity was $\overline{\mathrm{AE}} = -0.84$ (SD $= 40.18$), with 48.2\% of scenarios producing positive $\mathrm{AE}$ (Table~\ref{tab:descriptives}).
Both the near-zero signed shift and the near-chance $\mathrm{AE}$ positive-rate indicate that, averaged over the broader multi-domain attack surface, exposure produced no systematic movement in the adversarial direction: substantial opinion change occurred, but in directions that were effectively random with respect to the adversarial goal.

The absence of a uniform boomerang pattern is a genuine departure from the single-domain predecessor study (Defence-only), in which three of four opinion leaves showed systematic resistance-direction shifts.
Per-leaf $\mathrm{AE}$ ranges were tight in the present design ($\overline{\mathrm{AE}}_\text{leaf} \in [-2.79, +1.30]$, all SDs $\in [39, 42]$), and absolute displacements were similar across all eight leaves ($\bar{|\Delta|} \in [33.87, 36.18]$; Table~\ref{tab:descriptives})---consistent with a ceiling on leaf-level heterogeneity once the opinion state space is sampled across unrelated policy domains.

\subsection{Variance Partitioning}

Intraclass correlation \citep{shrout1979intraclass} at the profile level was $\mathrm{ICC}(1) = 0.000$ for absolute displacement ($\sigma^2_\mathrm{between} = 0.00$, $\sigma^2_\mathrm{within} = 409.06$, $k = 80$ profiles, cluster size $= 48.0$), $0.000$ for signed displacement ($\sigma^2_\mathrm{within} = 1{,}618.03$), and $0.000$ for adversarial effectivity ($\sigma^2_\mathrm{within} = 1{,}616.00$).
Point-estimated between-profile variance collapses to zero across all three outcomes in the present analysis.

This variance structure sets a hard upper bound on profile-level moderation in the broader design: essentially none of the scenario-level variance in opinion shift magnitude or direction partitions to stable between-profile differences once a single profile is observed across 48 unrelated attack--opinion task cells.
The result is consistent with persuasion research showing that context-level factors dominate individual differences \citep{matz2017psychological}, and it motivates treating the attack--opinion task rather than the profile as the primary unit of moderation analysis.

\subsection{Attack--Opinion Task Heterogeneity}

Across the 48 attack--opinion task cells, ridge regression was fit separately for each cell (5-fold cross-validated $\alpha$ grid; five $\alpha$ candidates in $[10^1, 10^{3.5}]$).
Cross-validated MSE ranged from $1{,}189.6$ (Fear Appeal $\times$ Alliance Commitment Support) to $1{,}969.9$ (Astroturf $\times$ Overseas Development Aid Support); reliability weights $w_t = n_t / \mathrm{CV\text{-}MSE}_t$ after normalisation spanned $\tilde{w} \in [0.017, 0.029]$, a much narrower range than the analogous 16-cell design in the single-domain predecessor study.
The narrower weight range is diagnostic: once a profile is observed across both conventional and AI-generated attacks on both Defence and non-Defence domains, no single task systematically distinguishes profiles from one another.

\subsection{Structural Equation Model}

The repeated-outcome SEM simultaneously regressed attack-averaged $\mathrm{AE}$ for each of the eight opinion leaves on six profile predictors (Neuroticism, Openness, Conscientiousness, Sex Female, Sex Other, mean baseline absolute score), with freely estimated residual covariances across all ${8 \choose 2} = 28$ indicator pairs.
Estimation used \texttt{semopy} with the default MLW estimator; the model converged with near-saturated fit: $\chi^2(21) = 0.025$, $p = 1.00$; CFI $= 1.000$; GFI $= 0.9997$; AGFI $= 0.9988$; TLI $= 1.000$; RMSEA $= 0.000$; AIC $= 168.0$; BIC $= 368.1$.
As in the single-domain predecessor study, the near-saturated fit indicates that at $N = 80$ the model is effectively unfalsifiable, and fit statistics should not be interpreted as confirmation of model quality.

No profile predictor reached $p < .05$ for any of the 48 leaf-specific paths; four paths reached $p < .10$ (all uncorrected): Integration Funding Support $\leftarrow$ Conscientiousness ($\hat\beta = -3.30$, $p = .061$), Trust in Mainstream Journalism $\leftarrow$ Conscientiousness ($\hat\beta = -2.99$, $p = .073$), Defense Industrial Reshoring Support $\leftarrow$ Neuroticism ($\hat\beta = -3.13$, $p = .095$), and Defense Industrial Reshoring Support $\leftarrow$ mean baseline absolute score ($\hat\beta = -3.21$, $p = .092$).
The Conscientiousness paths share the negative sign observed in the single-domain predecessor study, but none survives FDR correction across the 48-path family.
Full SEM path coefficients are in Supplementary Table~S5.

\subsection{Profile Moderator Analysis with FDR Correction}

The profile-level OLS model (outcome: mean $\mathrm{AE}$ per profile, $n = 80$) with HC3 heteroscedasticity-robust errors explained $R^2 = 0.151$ (Adj.\ $R^2 = 0.055$; $F(8, 71) = 1.612$, $p = .137$).
The model was not jointly significant.

No predictor survived Benjamini--Hochberg FDR correction \citep{benjamini1995controlling} across the moderator family (Table~\ref{tab:multivariate}).
Extraversion was the only predictor whose block-bootstrap 95\% CI excluded zero ($\hat\beta = +1.61$, SE $= 0.71$, $p = .023$; bootstrap 95\% CI $[0.47, 3.00]$; $B = 600$).
Conscientiousness retained the negative sign observed in the single-domain predecessor study but attenuated and fell below significance ($\hat\beta = -1.06$, $p = .159$; bootstrap 95\% CI $[-2.46, 0.30]$); Neuroticism ($\hat\beta = -0.86$, $p = .220$), Openness ($\hat\beta = +0.13$, $p = .862$), Agreeableness ($\hat\beta = -0.01$, $p = .989$), both sex dummies, and mean baseline absolute score were all non-significant at any conventional level.

The positive Extraversion coefficient indicates that more outgoing, socially expressive profiles showed nominally higher adversarial effectivity scores in the multi-domain design---a susceptibility-amplifying direction.
Because the CI excludes zero only marginally at the unadjusted threshold, because this predictor was not pre-registered as a primary hypothesis, and because the moderator-family FDR adjustment returns $q > .05$, we treat Extraversion as an exploratory finding in need of replication, not a confirmed moderator.

\subsection{Hierarchical Importance Decomposition}

Leave-one-group-out cross-validated $R^2$ decomposition computed marginal contributions of 20 ontology-aligned feature groups to the conditional susceptibility index.
Full model CV-$R^2 \approx -0.028$, confirming that out-of-sample predictive power is essentially nil at $N = 80$: overfitting cost exceeds in-sample explained variance by a small margin.

Within-sample relative importance (absolute marginal $R^2$, normalised) attributed the largest shares to the Big Five collectively (22.1\%), the Political Engagement Inventory (10.1\%), the Need for Cognition dimension of the Dual Process Inventory (8.9\%), the Platform Mechanism Awareness dimension of the Digital Literacy Inventory (7.3\%), and smaller shares to individual Big Five facets (Openness 5.8\%; Extraversion 5.8\%; Conscientiousness 0.1\%).
Demographics contributed 2.7\% of normalised relative importance.
The near-zero CV-$R^2$ emphasises that the susceptibility index should be interpreted as a conditional relative ranking within the present design and its configured target set, not an absolute predictive instrument; validation against held-out or human data is required (see Construct Validity).

\subsection{Bootstrap Confidence Intervals on Susceptibility Rankings}

Profile-level block bootstrap resampling ($B = 600$) yielded 95\% confidence intervals on all primary OLS coefficients (Table~\ref{tab:multivariate}; full parameters in \texttt{bootstrap\_primary\_params.csv}).
Only the Extraversion interval excluded zero ($[0.47, 3.00]$, bootstrap mean $1.66$, bootstrap SD $0.66$); all other coefficients had bootstrap CIs overlapping zero.
At the profile rank level, percentile-ranked susceptibility indices clustered narrowly around the median, with the top five most-susceptible profiles showing mean $\mathrm{AE}$ between $+0.08$ and $+10.67$---an order of magnitude smaller than the leaf-level standard deviations, confirming that profile-level susceptibility rankings are descriptive rather than predictive at the current study scale.

\section{Discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Attack--Opinion Context Dominates Profile-Level Variance}

The collapse of $\mathrm{ICC}(1)$ to effectively zero across all three outcomes (absolute displacement, signed displacement, adversarial effectivity) in the present analysis confirms that the combination of attack vector and opinion leaf is the primary determinant of outcome variance once the opinion state space is sampled across multiple policy domains: no detectable share of scenario-level variance partitions to stable between-profile differences.
This is a stronger version of the result from the single-domain predecessor study ($\mathrm{ICC}(1) = 0.052$ for absolute displacement), and it aligns with the persuasion literature showing that psychologically targeted content produces modest average individual-level effects \citep{matz2017psychological,bail2018exposure}.
It has a direct operational implication: the same individual may resist one attack on one issue while being substantially moved by a different combination, and aggregate profile-based susceptibility classification therefore has limited utility as a \textit{universal} vulnerability score.
The appropriate framing is \textit{conditional susceptibility} indexed to specific attack--opinion contexts.

Unlike the single-domain predecessor study, the present broader design did not exhibit a systematic boomerang pattern across domains: mean $\mathrm{AE}$ per leaf stayed within $\pm 3$ units of zero, with no leaf producing a directionally large effect in either the adversarial or resistance direction.
Rather than a substantive claim that adversarial attacks ``work no better than chance'', this pattern most plausibly reflects that the previously observed Defence-domain boomerang was an interaction between a particular adversarial-direction assignment and strongly anchored pro-defense positions in the underlying LLM---an interaction that does not generalise once non-defence domains are added to the target set.

\subsection{Non-Replication of the Conscientiousness Signal}

The most consequential scientific result of the present study is that the Conscientiousness effect observed in the single-domain predecessor study ($\hat\beta = -3.26$, $p = .004$, bootstrap 95\% CI $[-5.10, -1.20]$, FDR $q = .030$) does \emph{not} replicate in the extended design.
In the present, broader multi-domain design, Conscientiousness retains its negative sign in both the OLS profile model ($\hat\beta = -1.06$, $p = .159$, bootstrap 95\% CI $[-2.46, 0.30]$) and in several leaf-specific SEM paths (Integration Funding $p = .061$; Trust in Mainstream Journalism $p = .073$), but the effect size attenuates by roughly two-thirds and the interval crosses zero.

Two factors plausibly contribute.
First, the present study halves the Defence-domain weight in the target set (from 4/4 leaves to 2/8) and adds AI-driven synthetic-media attacks that did not appear in the single-domain predecessor study; if the protective effect of deliberative processing is strongest against traditional narrative framing on defence policy---as hinted by the predecessor study's leaf-specific path coefficients---dilution across heterogeneous tasks will attenuate a profile-level average.
Second, $N = 80$ profiles leaves the design underpowered for detecting a predecessor-study-magnitude effect after FDR correction across 8 moderators and 48 leaf-specific SEM paths; the $R^2 = 0.151$ and $F(8, 71) = 1.612$, $p = .137$ overall model indicates that the design provides weak discrimination between any of the profile predictors.

We therefore do not treat Conscientiousness as a disconfirmed moderator; we treat it as not replicated at FDR-corrected significance in a broader but less powered design.
The appropriate inference is that \emph{single-domain estimates of personality--susceptibility associations do not straightforwardly generalise to broader attack and opinion surfaces}, and that larger profile panels ($N \gtrsim 500$) are required to detect predecessor-magnitude moderators once the test family is FDR-corrected across a multi-domain, multi-attack design.

\subsection{Extraversion as an Exploratory Susceptibility-Amplifying Finding}

The one predictor whose bootstrap 95\% CI excludes zero in the present analysis---Extraversion ($\hat\beta = +1.61$, $p = .023$, CI $[0.47, 3.00]$)---was not pre-registered as a primary moderator and was not significant in the single-domain predecessor study ($\hat\beta = -2.35$, $p = .130$, with the opposite sign).
The sign reversal across the two designs and the failure to survive FDR correction both argue against treating this as a confirmed finding.
A plausible exploratory interpretation is that Extraversion, which captures social engagement, assertiveness, and sociability \citep{mccrae1997personality}, may increase receptivity to social-consensus and astroturfing-style attacks in contexts where the adversarial direction is aligned with socially shared positions.
This interpretation is tentative and requires both replication and domain-conditional analysis.

\subsection{Digital Literacy and AI-Based Attacks: No Detectable Domain-Specific Effects}

The present study included two AI-driven attack vectors (Deepfake Audio Speech Impersonation, LLM Chatbot Personalised Persuasion) and a new Information Integrity opinion domain (Counter Disinformation Agency Support, Trust in Mainstream Journalism), and extended the profile ontology with Digital Literacy and Dual Process Inventories.
The a-priori hypothesis, derived from experimental evidence on AI-personalised content \citep{goldstein2023generative,roozenbeek2022psychological,kozyreva2020citizens,guess2021digital}, was that Digital Literacy dimensions would show protective effects specifically for Information Integrity leaves targeted by synthetic media and AI-personalised attacks.

This hypothesis is not supported in the present analysis.
The Digital Literacy Inventory accounts for only 2.2\% of normalised relative importance in the hierarchical decomposition (with a Platform Mechanism Awareness sub-dimension at 7.3\%), and neither the Trust in Mainstream Journalism nor the Counter Disinformation Agency Support leaf produces leaf-specific SEM paths reaching $p < .05$ for any Digital Literacy or Dual Process Inventory predictor.
The most restrained interpretation is that the present sample size is insufficient to detect domain-conditional literacy effects once the test family is FDR-corrected, not that such effects are absent.
A confirmatory test would require at least an order of magnitude more profiles and explicit pre-registration of the domain-conditional hypothesis.

\subsection{Construct Validity}

The simulation pipeline faces three construct validity constraints that must be stated explicitly.

First, \textit{synthetic profiles are not humans}. LLM-generated pseudoprofiles produce conditioned responses that approximate human response distributions at aggregate levels \citep{argyle2023out} but are not validated against individual-level criterion data. Personality--susceptibility associations in the simulation may diverge from human experimental findings in magnitude, direction, or both. Criterion validation against human participants exposed to analogous attack content is the necessary next step.

Second, \textit{generated attack content is not real attack traffic}. The pipeline generates plausible platform-native manipulative content, audited for realism, but it is generated in a controlled context rather than collected from live adversarial operations. The attack content distribution may not match what adversaries actually deploy, and effectiveness estimates may be biased toward or against any specific attack type as a result.

Third, \textit{adversarial-direction assignments embed researcher assumptions}. Each opinion leaf is assigned a fixed adversarial goal direction ($d_k \in \{-1, +1\}$), operationalising a specific assumption about adversarial intent. Changing direction assignments would change adversarial effectivity scores. The assignments in the present study reflect a theoretically motivated model of what adversaries in the defence and geopolitical space are likely to pursue, but they should be made explicit and subjected to external review for any operational application.

\subsection{Limitations}

The cross-validated $R^2$ for the conditional susceptibility index is near zero ($\approx -0.028$) at the present scale ($N = 80$ profiles), confirming that aggregate profile-level prediction is effectively nil out-of-sample in the broader multi-domain design.
In-sample $R^2 = 0.151$ is both small in absolute terms and not jointly significant against the null ($F(8, 71) = 1.612$, $p = .137$); the present study is best understood as a methodology and infrastructure demonstration rather than a definitive moderation study.
Standard errors do not fully account for the clustered structure of repeated observations per profile; profile-level block bootstrap CIs ($B = 600$) are provided as a partial remedy \citep{cameron2008bootstrap}.
The SEM fit indices ($\chi^2(21) = 0.025$, $p = 1.0$; CFI $= 1.0$; RMSEA $= 0.0$) approach perfect-fit territory, indicating the model is near-saturated at $N = 80$; fit statistics should not be interpreted as confirmation of model quality at this scale.
The factorial design uses only attacked scenarios; a no-attack control condition would strengthen causal inference about exposure versus baseline opinion drift.
The $\mathrm{ICC}(1) \approx 0$ estimate indicates that, with the current profile and task panel, no detectable between-profile variance in opinion shift survives the within-profile task-level noise; this is itself a substantive limitation on the feasibility of profile-level susceptibility discrimination in designs of comparable breadth and scale.
The apparent non-replication of the Conscientiousness signal from the single-domain predecessor study is consistent with both a true attenuation of the effect across domains and a power-limited failure to detect an effect of similar magnitude; further studies with $N \gtrsim 500$ profiles and explicit domain stratification are required to distinguish these explanations.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The present study ($N = 3{,}840$ scenarios across 80 profiles, 6 attack vectors including two AI-driven synthetic-media attacks, 8 opinion leaves spanning four top-level domains) demonstrates that a full factorial, ontology-driven simulation produces an internally consistent, analytically decomposable measurement architecture for studying susceptibility to adversarial opinion manipulation.
The central empirical result is negative: no profile-level predictor survives FDR correction across the moderator family, the between-profile variance component collapses to zero for all three outcomes, and the Conscientiousness signal observed in the single-domain predecessor study (Defence-only) does not replicate at FDR-corrected significance in the broader multi-domain, AI-inclusive design.
Extraversion emerges as the only predictor whose bootstrap 95\% CI excludes zero ($\hat\beta = +1.61$, CI $[0.47, 3.00]$), but with the opposite sign to the predecessor study and without pre-registration or FDR-corrected significance, and it is treated as exploratory.

This pattern is methodologically informative.
It suggests that single-domain personality--susceptibility claims are fragile to design breadth: the apparent dominance of Conscientiousness in the single-domain predecessor study was either a domain-specific effect that attenuates once non-defence issue domains are included, or an effect of sufficient magnitude that a power increase to $N \gtrsim 500$ profiles is required to detect it under FDR correction in a multi-domain family.
Both interpretations have direct implications for operational susceptibility auditing: any deployed profile-based vulnerability classifier must be validated on the specific attack--opinion surface it is claimed to characterise, not extrapolated from narrower single-domain studies.

The five principal contributions of the pipeline infrastructure are:
(1) adversarial effectivity as a directional primary outcome, distinguishing successful manipulation from resistance and boomerang effects;
(2) conditional susceptibility indexing with cross-task ridge aggregation and reliability-weighted task contributions;
(3) profile-level block-bootstrap confidence intervals quantifying uncertainty in profile coefficients and susceptibility rankings;
(4) a fully generic CLI-accessible pipeline supporting user-defined ontologies for deployment in custom threat contexts, including an extended profile ontology spanning Big Five, Dual Process, Digital Literacy, and Political Engagement inventories;
(5) an extended attack taxonomy covering AI-generated synthetic media and personalised LLM persuasion alongside four conventional social-media attack mechanisms.

Substantive claims about real populations require validation against human experimental data; the present study provides the methodological and software infrastructure for that comparison.
The pipeline is publicly available and designed for modular extension to additional attack categories, psychological inventories, and opinion domains as the threat landscape evolves.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section*{Dual-Use Ethics Statement}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This research models mechanisms of cognitive vulnerability to adversarial persuasion.
The primary intended application is \textbf{defensive}: identifying population segments for which particular attack--opinion combinations produce elevated susceptibility, enabling targeted inoculation, media literacy interventions, and institutional protection strategies.

We acknowledge the dual-use risk: the same susceptibility estimates that enable defence also characterise exploitable targets.
We mitigate this risk through the following design decisions:
(i) all profiles are synthetic and not linked to identifiable individuals;
(ii) the output of the pipeline is population-level susceptibility patterns indexed to psychographic profile dimensions, not a tool for identifying and targeting specific individuals;
(iii) the pipeline outputs conditional estimates that are weak at the individual level (near-zero aggregate $R^2$), limiting precision-targeting utility;
(iv) the code and methodology are published openly to enable independent validation, critique, and controlled deployment.

Any deployment in an operational context should be subject to institutional ethics review, restricted to defence-oriented applications, and should explicitly prohibit use for adversarial population targeting.

\section*{Methods}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection*{Ontology-Constrained Factorial Design}

The present study and the single-domain predecessor study use the same three hierarchical JSON ontologies; the two designs differ only in which leaves enter the factorial design.

\textbf{PROFILE ontology.}
Continuous leaves are expressed as percentile scores (0--100).
The present study uses 85 feature dimensions: Big Five personality (NEO-PI-R structure; five traits $\times$ 4--6 facets), chronological age, sex (categorical with three levels), and three additional psychometric inventories---the Dual Process Inventory (analytical thinking, cognitive effort, intuitive override, need for cognition), the Digital Literacy Inventory (critical evaluation, source triangulation, algorithmic awareness, platform mechanism awareness, misinformation detection), and the Political Engagement Inventory (news engagement, political efficacy, collective efficacy beliefs, civic participation, institutional trust, ideological openness).
The single-domain predecessor study used the 42-dimension Big Five + demographics subset of the same ontology.

\textbf{ATTACK ontology (18 leaves total).}
Leaves are organised under four mechanism families:
Social Media Misinformation (Misleading Narrative Framing; Fabricated Statistic Claim; Selective Historical Analogy; Strawman Quote Fabrication);
Coordinated Amplification (Astroturf Comment Wave; Bot-Like Repetition Burst; Review Bombing Cascade; Cross-Platform Narrative Laundering);
Manipulative Persuasion (Fear Appeal Scapegoating Post; Ingroup--Outgroup Identity Priming; Pseudo Expert Authority Cue; False Flag Accusation; Urgency--Scarcity Priming);
Synthetic Media Generation (Deepfake Audio Speech Impersonation; AI-Generated Image Event Fabrication; LLM Chatbot Personalized Persuasion).
The present study selects six leaves: Misleading Narrative Framing, Fear Appeal Scapegoating Post, Astroturf Comment Wave, Pseudo Expert Authority Cue, LLM Chatbot Personalized Persuasion, Deepfake Audio Speech Impersonation.
The single-domain predecessor study selected the first four.

\textbf{OPINION ontology (62 leaves total).}
Leaves are organised under four top-level domains:
Immigration and Citizenship (17 leaves);
Foreign Policy and Geopolitics (17 leaves);
Defence and National Security (16 leaves);
Information Integrity and Platforms (12 leaves).
Each leaf carries a pre-specified adversarial direction $d_k \in \{-1, +1\}$ encoding the operator's intended opinion movement.
Of 62 leaves, 57 carry non-zero adversarial direction.
The present study samples 8 leaves spanning all four domains (two per domain: Alliance Commitment Support and Defense Industrial Reshoring Support from Defence; Diplomacy First Preference and Overseas Development Aid Support from Foreign Policy; Birthright Citizenship Support and Integration Funding Support from Immigration; Counter Disinformation Agency Support and Trust in Mainstream Journalism from Information Integrity).
The single-domain predecessor study sampled 4 leaves from Defence and National Security only.

\subsection*{Profile Generation}

Profiles are generated deterministically by stratified sampling across the joint continuous-attribute space using percentile grids (profile\_candidate\_multiplier = 5).
Categorical attributes (sex, education level, news diet) are assigned by nearest-neighbour matching in the continuous space.
No LLM calls are used for profile generation; this ensures exact reproducibility and eliminates generation-side confounding.

\subsection*{Baseline and Post-Exposure Opinion Elicitation}

For each profile $i$, attack $a$, and opinion leaf $k$:
(1) Baseline opinion is elicited via a structured role-playing prompt encoding the profile's full attribute vector, the opinion leaf's domain context, and a request for a self-reported opinion score on the $[-1000, +1000]$ scale.
(2) Attack content is generated as a platform-native manipulative message tailored to the specific attack--opinion--profile combination, with direction-aware persuasion goals derived from $d_k$ and the profile's personality moderator values.
(3) Post-exposure opinion is elicited with the same scoring prompt after presenting the attack content.

All LLM calls use Mistral Small (mistralai/mistral-small-3.2-24b-instruct) via the OpenRouter API at temperature $= 0.15$ with up to two realism/coherence repair iterations.

\subsection*{Adversarial Effectivity and Primary Outcome}

Let $d_k \in \{-1, +1\}$ denote the pre-specified adversarial direction for leaf $k$.
The signed shift for profile $i$ under attack $a$ on leaf $k$ is $\Delta_{iak} = \text{post}_{iak} - \text{baseline}_{iak}$, and adversarial effectivity is $\mathrm{AE}_{iak} = \Delta_{iak} \times d_k$.
Positive $\mathrm{AE}$ means the attack moved the opinion in the adversarially intended direction; negative values indicate resistance or backfire.

\subsection*{Conditional Susceptibility Index}

For each attack--opinion task $t = (a, k)$, a ridge regression model is fit on the $n_t$ profile observations:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\beta}_t = \arg\min_\beta \sum_i (\mathrm{AE}_{iak} - x_i^\top\beta)^2 + \alpha_t \|\beta_{-0}\|_2^2,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_t$ is selected by 5-fold cross-validation.
The reliability weight for task $t$ is $w_t = n_t / \mathrm{CV\text{-}MSE}_t$.
The conditional susceptibility index for profile $i$ over target set $\mathcal{T}$ is the percentile rank of the reliability-weighted predicted adversarial effectivity:
\begin{equation}
S_i^*(\mathcal{T}) = \operatorname{PctRank}\!\left(\sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \tilde{w}_t \, x_i^\top\hat{\beta}_t\right), \quad \tilde{w}_t = w_t \Big/ \sum_{t'} w_{t'}.
\end{equation}

Optional cross-task partial pooling (Empirical-Bayes shrinkage, $\lambda \in [0,1]$) shrinks each task's coefficient vector toward the reliability-weighted cross-task mean, reducing overfitting when a task has few observations.
Block bootstrap ($B = 600$ resamples at profile level) provides 90\% confidence intervals on percentile ranks and cross-task coefficient standard deviations.

\subsection*{Hierarchical Importance Decomposition}

Feature groups are discovered from column names via prefix trie (no hardcoded inventory names).
For each group $g$, marginal cross-validated $R^2$ is $\Delta R^2_g = R^2_{\text{full}} - R^2_{\text{ablated}\,g}$, where the ablated model omits group $g$'s columns.
Relative importance is $|\Delta R^2_g| / \sum_{g'} |\Delta R^2_{g'}| \times 100$.

\subsection*{Structural Equation Model}

A profile-level repeated-outcome SEM \citep{rosseel2012lavaan,mackinnon2007mediation} simultaneously regresses attack-averaged adversarial effectivity for each opinion leaf on profile moderators, with freely estimated residual covariances.
Estimation uses OLS with HC3 heteroscedasticity-robust covariance (profile-level bootstrap standard errors for key paths).
FDR correction uses the Benjamini--Hochberg procedure \citep{benjamini1995controlling} applied to all moderator p-values.
